Driver Spotlight

From 1:28 to 1:12. Dominic Borelli’s 16-Second Season.

February 28, 2026 — BRKC Rounds 2, 3, 5 & 6

← Back to News
Driver Spotlight February 28, 2026 By Chris Haynes, Marketing Manager | Written with the help of AI to process the race data

Not every story at the Lorain Ohio KartPlex is about who crossed the finish line first. Some of the best ones are about who showed up, kept at it, and got better. A lot better.

Dominic Borelli is 8 years old. He races the #25 kart under the Borelli Motorsports banner in the T4 Mini class at BRKC. He’s never won a race against the 206 Mini/Cadet field. He’s never stood on the top step of the overall podium. But what he did across four rounds of the 2025 season is one of the most impressive improvement arcs in the entire BRKC dataset.

He dropped nearly 16 seconds off his best lap time. At 8 years old.

Round 2: The Starting Line (September 6, 2025)

Borelli’s first timed lap of the BRKC season was a 1:34.224 in qualifying. He missed practice entirely (DNS), so when he rolled out for qualifying it was cold. No warm-up laps, no reference point. Just an 8-year-old figuring it out in real time.

By his third qualifying lap he was down to a 1:28.384. That was 16.3 seconds behind the only other driver in the class, Aiden Harbaugh, who was running 1:12s in the 206 Mini/Cadet kart. Different engine, different class, but same track and same session.

The Prefinal was better. He posted a 1:23.440 on lap 6, nearly five seconds faster than his qualifying best. But consistency was a problem. His laps ranged from 1:23.4 to 1:27.7. In the Final, the spread got even wider: his best was a 1:24.011, but his last lap was a 1:48.812. A 24.8-second gap between his fastest and slowest laps in a single race.

He finished every race. He was a full lap behind Harbaugh in both the Prefinal and Final. But he was on track, completing laps, and getting faster as the day went on. For a first BRKC outing at 8 years old, that counts.

Round 3: Small Gains, Big Consistency (September 7, 2025)

Just one day later, Borelli was back. This time he made it out for practice, running two laps and posting a 1:33.625. Not exactly a blistering pace, but he was on track early and building a rhythm.

Qualifying went smoother. His three laps were tightly grouped: 1:28.672, 1:28.887, 1:27.274, with the final lap being his quickest. No wild swings.

The real story was the Final. His best lap was a 1:23.295, just 0.145 seconds faster than his Round 2 personal best. A tiny gain on paper. But the consistency improvement was massive.

His lap time spread dropped from 24.8 seconds to 4.6 seconds. Same driver, same kart, one day later. He went from all-over-the-place to running laps that were within a few seconds of each other. That’s not about raw speed. That’s about learning how to drive a race.

Round 5: The Breakthrough (October 12, 2025)

Five weeks passed between Rounds 3 and 5. Whatever Borelli did during that break, it worked.

He showed up and ran a 1:23.318 in his first practice session. About the same as where he left off at Round 3. Normal.

Then he went out for Practice 2 and posted a 1:20.305. Three seconds faster, just like that. Then in qualifying he dropped to 1:16.969. Then in Heat 1 he hit 1:14.674.

That’s an 8.6-second improvement within a single round. From 1:23 in the morning to 1:14 by the afternoon.

The field was also much bigger this time. Eight drivers in the combined Mini/Cadet class, including Anthony Ciofani, Logan Pollack, Aiden Harbaugh, and Ashlyn Taylor. These are some of the fastest Mini/Cadet drivers in the BRKC. And while Borelli was still a lap behind the 206 leaders, the gap had narrowed significantly. At Round 2, his best Final lap was 12.3 seconds slower than the fastest 206 driver. At Round 5, that gap was down to 6.1 seconds. Nearly halved.

His consistency held up too. The Final had a lap time spread of just 3.4 seconds, and his two fastest laps came in the second half of the race. No fade. No dramatic slow-down on the last lap. Just solid, clean racing.

Round 6: P1 Qualifier (October 18, 2025)

For Round 6, the T4 Mini/Swift Mini class was separated from the 206 Mini/Cadet group. That put Borelli in a three-kart field alongside Remy Boss (Swift class) and Austin Harbaugh (T4 Mini).

Qualifying was the highlight. Borelli went out and posted a 1:12.524 on his third lap. P1. His first ever pole position. He beat Austin Harbaugh by 3.6 seconds.

Think about that for a second. The same driver who ran a 1:28.384 in qualifying at Round 2 was now qualifying first with a 1:12.524. That’s a 15.860-second improvement in qualifying pace across four rounds.

Remy Boss won the overall races in his Swift class kart, but among T4 Mini drivers, Borelli was clearly the strongest. He lapped Austin Harbaugh in the Final and ran six of his eight laps under 1:14.1.

From 1:28.384 to 1:12.524. Nearly 16 seconds. His consistency went from a 24.8-second spread in the Final to under 4 seconds. The gap to the 206 class leaders shrank from over 12 seconds to about 6. And by Round 6, he was the fastest T4 Mini driver on the grid.

Why This Matters

Borelli’s season is the kind of story that doesn’t show up on a podium. He never beat the 206 Mini/Cadet leaders. He was always a lap or more behind the front-runners. If you just looked at final positions, you’d never know what was happening.

But the timing sheets tell a different story. They show a driver who showed up four times, got faster every single round, learned how to be consistent, and ended the season running laps that would have been unimaginable at Round 2. All at 8 years old.

That’s what BRKC is built for. Not every driver is going to win races in their first season. Some are going to spend their first year figuring out where the braking zones are, learning how to hold a line, and trying to keep the kart pointed the right direction. The ones who stick with it and keep coming back are the ones who end up surprising everyone.

Dominic Borelli dropped nearly 16 seconds in one season. If he keeps improving at that rate, the rest of the T4 Mini field is going to have a problem on their hands in 2026.
